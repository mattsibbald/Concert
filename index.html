Mozart: Sonata K.448 & Concerto K.365

The two-piano masterworks of a genius — written at ages 25 and 23
🎵 About the Sonata (K.448)

Mozart wrote this sparkling sonata in 1781 for himself and his student Josepha Auernhammer. The two pianos chase and mirror each other like two friends in a playful conversation. Written the same year Mozart moved to Vienna to seek fame and fortune at age 25, this piece radiates pure joy and confidence.

🎵 About the Concerto (K.365)

Written two years earlier for Mozart and his sister Nannerl — this is the only piano concerto ever written for two pianists! Mozart and Nannerl had grown up performing together as a duo-prodigy act across Europe. This was their ultimate show-off piece, designed to dazzle audiences with perfect sibling synchrony.

🧠 The "Mozart Effect"

In 1993, scientists found college students showed brief improvements in spatial reasoning after listening to K.448. Newspapers went wild — parents started playing Mozart to their babies! While the effect is modest, this Sonata became the most studied piece of music in history. Whether or not it makes you smarter, it definitely sounds amazing.

⚡ Big Idea: Child Prodigies

Mozart's father Leopold recognised his son's gift early and devoted his life to promoting him. By age 6, Mozart had performed for Empress Maria Theresa in Vienna and King Louis XV in France. But was this childhood good for Mozart? Some historians think the constant pressure and travel wore him out — he died exhausted at 35.

🔬 Tonight's Connection The two Hamelins take the very same "two pianists in conversation" format Mozart invented and bring it to a modern audience. Watch how they look at each other — musical communication is a real, physical, visible thing!

Chopin: Rondo in C Major, Op. 73

A piece Chopin never lived to hear performed — published 6 years after his death
🎵 What is a Rondo?

A Rondo is a piece where a main theme keeps coming back like a refrain in a song — ABACADA... The two pianos take turns presenting and answering the theme. Chopin probably wrote this early in his career for private salon concerts with a friend. It's lively and fun — very different from the melancholy Chopin most people know.

🏆 The Chopin Competition

The International Chopin Piano Competition is held every 5 years in Warsaw — considered the world's most prestigious piano competition. In 2015, Charles Richard-Hamelin (performing tonight!) won the Silver Medal there. So when he plays Chopin tonight, it is deeply, personally significant.

🇵🇱 Poland's National Hero

Chopin left Poland at 20 and never returned, but was fiercely patriotic. His Mazurkas and Polonaises are symbols of Polish identity. During World War II, the Nazis banned Chopin's music in occupied Poland because it was a rallying symbol of national resistance and spirit.

💊 Living with Illness

Chopin had tuberculosis — a serious lung disease — most of his life. It made him frail and affected his ability to perform and compose. Despite this, he created music of extraordinary depth and beauty. His illness is part of why his music is so emotional — he lived with the constant knowledge that he might not have long.

🌟 Tonight's Connection Charles Richard-Hamelin is Chopin's greatest Canadian champion. Tonight you're watching a Chopin Competition Silver Medalist perform Chopin's own music — it doesn't get more personal or meaningful than this!
💫

Chaminade: Pas des Cymbales, Op. 36

A dance piece from a woman who had to fight to be taken seriously
🎵 What Does "Pas des Cymbales" Mean?

"Pas des Cymbales" means "Dance of the Cymbals" in French. It's a ballet-style piece — elegant and sparkling, evoking the shimmer and clash of cymbals. Chaminade was famous for writing beautiful, accessible music that people actually enjoyed. This piece is from her collection "Les Sylvains," inspired by woodland spirit dancers.

✊ Women in Classical Music

In Chaminade's time, it was extremely unusual for a woman to be taken seriously as a composer. Women were expected to play music at home, not write it for concert halls. Chaminade fought these prejudices her entire life. In 1908 she toured America — where audiences were more open-minded — and was celebrated like a star.

📊 The Numbers Today

Even now in 2026, classical music has a gender gap problem. Studies show only about 5–10% of pieces performed by major orchestras are by women composers. Organisations around the world are actively pushing to programme more music by women and underrepresented composers. Chaminade's inclusion tonight is part of that important effort.

🎖️ Her Legacy

Chaminade lived to 86, surviving two World Wars, seeing her music go from enormously popular to nearly forgotten — and then rediscovered. Today she's celebrated as a pioneering figure. Her music appearing in a major concert programme like tonight's is a deliberate, meaningful statement about whose music deserves to be heard.

💡 Think About This How many female composers can you name? Most people can name dozens of male composers but struggle to name women. This isn't because women didn't write great music — it's because their music was often ignored, unpublished, or credited to men. Tonight is a small but important correction to that long history.
🐻

Medtner: Two Pieces for Two Pianos, Op. 58

Music for loyal hearts — from a composer who refused to follow fashion
🎵 What to Listen For

Medtner's music sounds rich, complex, and deeply romantic. His two-piano pieces are intimate conversations rather than competitions. Listen for the long, singing melodies and how the two pianos seem to be sharing a secret. His music rewards careful listening — there's always something beautiful happening beneath the surface.

🤝 Medtner & Rachmaninoff

Sergei Rachmaninoff (one of the most famous pianists and composers ever) and Medtner were close friends who deeply admired each other. When Rachmaninoff became world-famous, Medtner remained a "composer's composer" — loved by musicians but unknown to the public. Rachmaninoff always said Medtner was the greatest living composer.

🌍 Revolution and Exile

In 1917, Russia's Communist Revolution transformed the country. The new government wanted artists to write simple, accessible music for the masses — Medtner refused. He composed complex, traditional works and wouldn't compromise. He eventually left Russia permanently, spending his final years in England, homesick and struggling financially.

🎵 The "Fairy Tale" Composer

Medtner invented a new piano form called the "Skazka" — Russian for "fairy tale." These short pieces are like musical stories, full of mystery and atmosphere. Marc-André Hamelin has recorded all of Medtner's Skazki and is considered one of the world's leading interpreters. Tonight is special — you're hearing Medtner played by its greatest living champion.

🎙️ Tonight's Connection Marc-André Hamelin has championed Medtner's music his entire career. He believes strongly in performing works by underappreciated composers — he's helped bring Medtner back to concert stages around the world. Tonight you're witnessing exactly that mission in action!
🎷

Gershwin: Fantasy on "Porgy and Bess"

Jazz, blues, and classical music fused — and a complex, important legacy
🎭 What is Porgy and Bess?

"Porgy and Bess" (1935) is an opera Gershwin called a "folk opera" — set in a fictional Black community in South Carolina. It contains some of the most beautiful songs in American music: "Summertime," "I Got Plenty o' Nothin'," "It Ain't Necessarily So." Percy Grainger's fantasy weaves these songs into a brilliant two-piano miniature of the whole opera.

🤔 A Complex Legacy

Porgy and Bess has always been controversial. Gershwin (who was white and Jewish) wrote an opera set in a Black community with an all-Black cast. Some Black artists and critics have questioned whether he truly understood Black culture, or whether some stereotypes are harmful. Others celebrate it as putting Black American music at the centre of the classical world. This debate continues today.

🎺 Jazz Meets Classical

Gershwin grew up hearing jazz and blues in New York — music created by Black Americans — and incorporated those sounds into formal concert music. His "Rhapsody in Blue" (1924) was the first piece to truly merge the two worlds. Tonight you'll hear that same electric energy in the Porgy and Bess fantasy.

🧩 Who Was Percy Grainger?

Percy Grainger (1882–1961) was an eccentric Australian-American composer who collected folk songs across England and Scandinavia. He was also one of the most unconventional people in music history — he ran everywhere instead of walking, rejected formal clothes, and invented his own words for musical terms. He created this two-piano fantasy in 1951.

🌟 Something to Think About When you listen tonight, you'll be hearing music that started as jazz and blues in Brooklyn, was written into an American opera, arranged for two pianos by an Australian, and is now being performed by two Canadians in Toronto. Music truly travels across time and borders!
